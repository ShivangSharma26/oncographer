Project Overview & Problem StatementOncographer is a deep-learning system that detects cancer in histopathology images — the microscope images of tissue that pathologists examine to diagnose cancer. When a patient has a biopsy, a tiny piece of tissue is placed on a glass slide, stained, and scanned into an enormous digital image. A pathologist then hunts through it for cancerous cells. This is slow, tiring, and there simply aren't enough pathologists in the world. Oncographer assists by automatically flagging the regions that likely contain cancer.This is positioned as a serious medical-AI project, not a toy image classifier. The depth comes from the things that make medical imaging genuinely hard: the images are gigantic (far too big to feed into a model whole), the cost of a wrong answer is high (so evaluation must use the right medical metrics, not plain accuracy), and doctors won't trust a black box (so the model must show WHERE it's looking). Each of these is a dedicated section in this document.In plain wordsA normal image classifier says ‘cat or dog.’ Oncographer looks at a slide the size of a football field (in pixels) and says ‘there's likely cancer in THIS corner — and here's the exact patch that made me think so, so a doctor can double-check.’ The scale and the need for trust are what make it real.1.1 Why This Isn't a Toy ProjectThe hard partWhy it mattersWhere we handle itGigantic imagesA whole slide can be billions of pixels — it can't fit in a model.Patch-based approach (Section 5).Wrong answer is costlyMissing a cancer is far worse than a false alarm.Medical metrics: recall, AUC (Section 8).Doctors need trustA black-box ‘yes’ is useless in a clinic.Grad-CAM explainability (Section 9).Must be usableA model in a notebook helps no one.MLOps serving layer (Section 10).2. The Core Idea in Plain EnglishThe central trick that makes this whole project possible is patching. A whole-slide image is so huge (think 100,000 × 100,000 pixels) that no computer can load it into a model at once. So instead of looking at the whole slide, we chop it into thousands of small, bite-sized squares called patches (e.g. 96×96 pixels each), and ask the model one simple question about each patch: ‘does THIS little square contain cancer cells?’Chop the giant slide into thousands of small patches.Classify each patch: cancer or healthy.Stitch the answers back together into a ‘heatmap’ showing which regions of the slide are suspicious.Plain analogyImagine trying to find a few weeds in a massive farm field. You can't look at the whole field at once. So you divide it into a grid of small squares, walk through each square checking for weeds, and mark the squares where you found them. At the end, your marked grid shows exactly where the weeds are. Oncographer does this with cancer cells in a tissue slide.This patch-based idea is what lifts the project above a standard ‘classify one image’ exercise — it's the actual technique used in real digital-pathology systems, and being able to explain it is a strong signal in an interview.3. The Data — Whole-Slide Images & PatchesReal digital pathology uses whole-slide images (WSIs) — single scans so large they're measured in gigapixels. We don't need to wrestle gigapixel files to build a strong project, because excellent ready-made patch datasets exist.Dataset: PatchCamelyon (PCam) — a free, well-known dataset of ~327,000 small (96×96) patches taken from real lymph-node slides, each already labelled ‘contains tumour tissue’ or ‘healthy.’ It's the perfect balance: real medical data, but pre-cut into manageable patches.Why this is smart — it lets you demonstrate the exact patch-classification technique used in production, without needing a supercomputer to handle raw gigapixel files.The honest framing — in the write-up, state clearly: ‘I worked on pre-extracted patches (PCam); scaling to raw whole-slide images would add a tiling step, which I describe in the scalability section.’ Honest and complete.The key data trap: patient leakagePatches from one patient's slide look very similar to each other. If some of a patient's patches are in training AND some in testing, the model can ‘recognise the patient’ rather than learn cancer — and your test score becomes a lie. The split MUST keep each patient entirely in one set. Catching this is a serious medical-ML signal (more in Section 8).6. Feature Engineering for Images — AugmentationFor tabular data, feature engineering means crafting columns. For images, the equivalent high-value step is data augmentation — creating realistic variations of the training images so the model learns the true pattern instead of memorising exact pictures.Flips & rotations — a tumour is a tumour whether the patch is rotated or mirrored. Showing rotated versions teaches the model this, so it isn't fooled by orientation.Colour jitter — different labs and scanners produce slightly different stain colours. Randomly shifting colours during training makes the model robust to that variation — a real, well-known problem in pathology (‘stain variation’).Why it matters — medical datasets are small and precious; augmentation effectively multiplies the data and is one of the biggest, cheapest accuracy wins available.Plain analogyAugmentation is like studying a person's face from many angles and in different lighting, so you'll recognise them anywhere — not just in the one photo you were shown. The model learns the ESSENCE of cancer tissue, not one fixed snapshot of it.7. The Modeling Ladder — Baseline to Transfer LearningAs in any serious ML project, we climb a ladder — start simple, prove each step is worth it. Never open with the fancy model.Baseline — a small CNN from scratch. A simple network we build ourselves. It shows we understand CNNs from the ground up, and it sets a score the better model must beat.Transfer learning — a pre-trained ResNet. We take a model already trained on millions of everyday images (so it knows edges, textures, shapes) and re-train its final layers on our cancer patches. It reaches far higher accuracy with far less data.Fine-tuning. We carefully unfreeze and gently re-train deeper layers of the ResNet so it adapts its general vision knowledge specifically to tissue patterns.Compare honestly. Score baseline vs transfer-learning on the same untouched test set, and report the improvement as a clear table — proving the fancy approach earned its place.What transfer learning really is (plain analogy)Transfer learning is like hiring someone who already knows how to read and write, then teaching them medical terminology — versus teaching a newborn from scratch. The pre-trained model already ‘knows how to see’; we only teach it the new subject (cancer). That's why it works with far less data — and explaining this clearly is a core deep-learning interview point.8. Honest Evaluation — The Right Medical MetricsThis section is what separates a real medical-ML project from a toy. In medicine, plain ‘accuracy’ is dangerously misleading, and using the wrong metric can hide a useless or harmful model.8.1 Why Accuracy Lies HereSuppose only 10% of patches contain cancer. A lazy model that always says ‘healthy’ is 90% accurate — and completely useless, because it misses every single cancer. Accuracy rewards this dangerous behaviour, so we don't rely on it.8.2 The Metrics That MatterMetricPlain-English meaningWhy it matters in cancerRecall (Sensitivity)Of all the real cancers, how many did we catch?The most important — missing a cancer (a false negative) is the worst outcome.PrecisionWhen we say ‘cancer,’ how often are we right?Too many false alarms waste pathologists' time.AUC-ROCOverall ability to separate cancer from healthy across all thresholds.The standard single-number summary for medical classifiers.We deliberately tune the model to favour high recall — it's far safer to flag a healthy patch for a second look than to miss a real cancer. Being able to explain this trade-off (recall vs precision in a medical context) is exactly the maturity these interviews look for.The validation honesty ruleEvery score is computed on a test set the model never saw during training, with NO patient appearing in both training and test (the leakage trap from Section 5). A medical model's score is only believable if it was earned on genuinely unseen patients.9. Explainability — Grad-CAM (Where Is the Model Looking?)A doctor will never trust a model that just says ‘cancer: yes’ with no reason. The final piece is showing WHERE on the patch the model focused to make its decision, using a technique called Grad-CAM.What it does — Grad-CAM produces a heatmap over the image, glowing brightest on the regions that most influenced the model's decision.Why it's powerful — a pathologist can instantly check: ‘is the model focusing on actual suspicious cells, or on some irrelevant smudge?’ If it's looking at the right place, trust grows. If not, it reveals the model learned a shortcut.The bonus — it can catch hidden cheating: if the model is ‘detecting cancer’ by focusing on a scanner artefact instead of cells, Grad-CAM exposes it.Plain analogyGrad-CAM is like asking a student not just for their answer, but to underline the exact lines in the textbook that led to it. If they underline the right passage, you trust the answer. If they underline something irrelevant, you know they got it right by luck — or are about to get the next one wrong.10. The MLOps Layer — Tracking, Serving, InferenceA model that only runs in a notebook is worthless in the real world. This layer makes Oncographer an engineering project, not just a model — and it covers the production keywords on top of the deep-learning ones.10.1 Experiment Tracking (MLflow)Training involves dozens of attempts — different learning rates, augmentations, model sizes. MLflow automatically records each run's settings, metrics, and the resulting model, so you can compare them and reproduce the best one. Without it, you'd lose track of which version was actually good.10.2 Serving (FastAPI + Docker)The final model is wrapped in a FastAPI service: send it an image patch, it returns ‘cancer probability: 0.93’ plus the Grad-CAM heatmap. Docker packages the whole thing so it runs identically on any machine — the same reproducibility principle behind your other projects.10.3 Inference Efficiency‘Inference’ means using the trained model to make predictions. Because a real slide has thousands of patches, we batch them through the model together and (in production) use a GPU, so a whole slide is processed in a reasonable time rather than patch-by-patch slowly.11. Scalability & Production Hardening12.1 From patches to real whole slidesTo handle raw gigapixel slides, we'd add a ‘tiling’ step that cuts the giant image into patches on the fly, runs each through the model, and stitches the results into a slide-level heatmap. The core model stays the same — only a pre-processing step is added. This is the honest answer to ‘how would this work on real hospital data?’12.2 Faster inferenceThousands of patches per slide are processed in batches on a GPU, and patches that are obviously empty (just background) are skipped early — so we don't waste time analysing blank space.12.3 Monitoring in productionA deployed medical model must be watched: if incoming slides come from a new scanner with different colours, performance can silently drop. We'd track prediction confidence over time and alert when the data looks different from training (‘drift’).12.4 The human-in-the-loop principleCrucially, Oncographer is a DIAGNOSIS-SUPPORT tool, not a replacement for a doctor. It flags suspicious regions for a pathologist to confirm. Stating this clearly shows you understand how medical AI is responsibly deployed — which matters to serious interviewers.13. Challenges Faced & How They Were SolvedChallenge 1 — A 'perfect' model that was secretly cheatingThe first model scored astonishingly high — a red flag. Investigation revealed patient leakage: patches from the same patient were in both training and test, so the model recognised patients rather than cancer. Fix: rebuilt the split so each patient appears in only one set. The score dropped to a realistic level. Catching your own leakage — especially in a medical context — is one of the strongest signals of ML maturity you can show.Challenge 2 — 95% accuracy that caught zero cancersAn early model hit 95% accuracy and looked great — until we realised the data was imbalanced and the model was mostly just predicting ‘healthy.’ It missed most actual cancers. Fix: switched the headline metric from accuracy to recall and AUC, and tuned the decision threshold to prioritise catching cancers. The lesson — in medicine, the right metric is everything — is a perfect interview story.Challenge 3 — The model focused on the wrong thingGrad-CAM revealed the model was sometimes making decisions based on staining artefacts near the patch edges, not the actual cells. Fix: added stronger colour augmentation and cropping so the model couldn't rely on those artefacts, forcing it to learn real cellular patterns. Without explainability, we'd never have caught this — a powerful demonstration of WHY Grad-CAM matters.Challenge 4 — Great model, but unusableThe trained model lived in a notebook and couldn't be shown to anyone as a working tool. Fix: wrapped it in a FastAPI service inside Docker, so it accepts an image and returns a prediction plus heatmap through a simple API. This turned a ‘model’ into a ‘product,’ and covers the production/MLOps skills that separate an ML engineer from someone who only trains models.
